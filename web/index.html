Lesson 01 · The velocity field

A fluid is a field of little arrows

Forget individual water molecules. In fluid dynamics we describe the flow by a velocity field: at every point in space there is an arrow, u(x, y), telling you how fast the fluid there is moving and in which direction.

Everything that follows is a rule for how this field of arrows changes from one instant to the next.

Lesson 02 · Advection

The flow carries things along with it

Drop a splash of dye into a river and it doesn't stay put — the current sweeps it downstream, stretching it into ribbons. That transport of stuff (and of the fluid's own momentum) by the flow is called advection.

In the equation

−(u⋅∇)u

The fluid advects itself: this term says the velocity is carried along by the velocity. It's what makes fluids swirl, and what makes them mathematically hard.

Lesson 03 · Vorticity

Straight-looking flow can still spin

Here the flow is perfectly horizontal — every arrow points sideways. But the top moves faster than the bottom (a shear), and that difference makes little paddle-wheels turn. That local spin is vorticity, ω = ∇×u.

Vorticity is where the beauty lives: smoke rings, whirlpools, and the eddies trailing off a canoe paddle are all concentrated vorticity.

Lesson 04 · Viscosity & diffusion

Friction smears sharp motion smooth

Real fluids have internal friction. A fast patch drags its slower neighbours along and gets dragged back — so sharp differences blur out over time. Mathematically that smoothing is the Laplacian, ∇², and how strongly it acts is the viscosity ν.

In the equation

+ν∇2u

The very same Laplacian that blurs this pattern also diffuses the fluid's momentum — that's the viscous term.

Lesson 05 · Pressure & incompressibility

You can't squash water into a corner

Water is (almost) incompressible: you can't pile more of it into a spot without the same amount leaving. In field terms, the flow must be divergence-free, ∇⋅u = 0. Whenever the flow tries to pile up, pressure instantly builds and pushes back to cancel it.

In the equation

−1ρ∇p

Solving for the pressure that keeps ∇⋅u = 0 everywhere is called the projection step — usually the most expensive part of a fluid simulator.

Lesson 06 · Putting it together

The Navier–Stokes equations

Stack the four effects you just met — advection carries the flow, viscosity smooths it, pressure keeps it incompressible, and your hand adds force — and you have the equations that govern nearly every fluid in the universe:

Conservation of momentum & mass

∂u∂t= −(u⋅∇)u −1ρ∇p +ν∇2u +f ∇⋅u=0

Advection · pressure · viscosity · your force — every wisp below is these terms solving themselves on a grid.
